# Retrospective format: What we stopped saying

## When to use this

Use this with mature teams that have been together for at least four PIs. Use it when the team has developed a vocabulary for what they discuss, and an unspoken vocabulary for what they do not. Signs that the format is appropriate:

- New team members notice things that the long-tenured members no longer notice.
- Some topics produce immediate humour or eye-rolls but no substantive discussion.
- Retrospectives surface the same three or four issues repeatedly, and no one is surprised.

The format is unsuitable for newly formed teams, teams under acute external pressure, and teams where one or more members have visibly low psychological safety. Running it in those contexts produces a thin version of the discussion that confirms the avoidance rather than addressing it.

Allow 75–90 minutes.

## What this is for

The format identifies topics that have become undiscussable, not because they were forbidden, but because they were once raised, did not produce change, and have since been quietly dropped. Undiscussable topics accumulate. Naming them is most of the work; resolving them is a separate effort.

## What you need

- A facilitator from outside the team. This format does not work with internal facilitation. The team's own members have all participated in the avoidance and cannot credibly invite the conversation.
- A blank wall and sticky notes, or a digital equivalent.
- An optional pre-session prompt sent two days in advance: *"Think of one topic that used to be raised in this team and is no longer raised. Bring it ready to describe."*

## Format

### Opening, 10 minutes

The facilitator names what the session is for, in plain language: *"Every team builds a list of things it has stopped saying. The list is not bad. It is part of how teams work. We are going to look at this team's list and decide which items, if any, are worth bringing back."*

Then read out the four reasons topics typically become undiscussable, without commentary:

1. Someone raised it, was not heard, and has stopped trying.
2. The topic touches a person whose feelings the team has decided to protect.
3. The topic is genuinely outside the team's control, and discussing it is dispiriting.
4. The topic was resolved once and the team has agreed not to reopen it.

State that all four are legitimate categories, and that the session will return to them.

### Activity one: silent listing, 15 minutes

Each participant writes, on sticky notes, topics they believe the team has stopped raising. One topic per note. Brief is better than complete.

The facilitator emphasises one rule: notes are anonymous and remain anonymous through the session.

Collect all notes. The facilitator reads them aloud, without commentary, in the order they were collected. Do not group, sort, or interpret yet.

### Activity two: categorisation, 15 minutes

The facilitator places each note into one of the four categories from the opening. The team can object to a placement; the facilitator moves the note. The team cannot debate the existence of a note.

Categorisation is not a vote. It is the facilitator making a judgement and the team correcting it.

### Activity three: the gentle return, 25 minutes

For category one (raised, not heard, dropped), the facilitator asks: *"Is there one topic on this list that someone in this room is willing to raise again, knowing the previous attempt did not produce change? If so, what would have to be different this time?"*

This question often produces silence. Allow the silence. Do not fill it.

If a topic is named, it becomes the subject of the next iteration's working conversation, scheduled before the session closes. Only one topic per session is moved forward. The discipline of moving one rather than three is part of the format.

For category two (protecting someone), the facilitator does not ask the team to name the person. The category is acknowledged and the conversation moves on. These topics require a different intervention, usually a private conversation between the facilitator or a senior person and the individual concerned.

For category three (outside the team's control), the facilitator asks: *"Is anyone in this room in a position to take this upward? If not, can we agree that it is closed for this team without pretending we have addressed it?"* Closure is acceptable; pretence is not.

For category four (previously resolved), the facilitator asks: *"Has anything changed since the resolution? If yes, the topic is open again. If no, it stays closed."*

### Closing, 10 minutes

The facilitator restates what was moved forward, what was acknowledged and closed, and what was acknowledged and parked. Nothing else.

The team does not produce action items. The action is the conversation that has just happened.

## Facilitation notes

The format can produce strong feelings late in the session, particularly when category-two items are named. Have a plan for how the facilitator handles a participant becoming visibly upset. The plan is usually: pause the session, offer a break, do not rush back. Resuming five minutes later is fine. Resuming after an emotion has been forced down is worse than ending the session early.

The facilitator should be prepared for a category-two topic to surface a person who is not in the room. The temptation to discuss them is strong and should be refused. Topics about people not in the room are not addressed in this format under any conditions.

A trap to avoid: collecting commitments from individual participants to "raise things more often". This sounds productive and is almost always counterproductive. The reasons topics became undiscussable are structural; commitments by individuals to be braver do not address the structure.

Another trap: declaring the session a success because difficult topics were named. Naming is necessary and not sufficient. The session is successful only if the one topic moved forward is engaged with seriously in the next two iterations. Track this. Report back to the team at the next retrospective on what happened.

## Pairing

Pair with the stakeholder-tension grid (`worksheets/stakeholder-tension-grid.md`) when category-two items appear to involve specific stakeholders, to externalise the relationship dynamics that are making the topic undiscussable.

## Variations

- **Half the team is remote.** Run the silent listing asynchronously over two days before the session. The categorisation and discussion remain live.
- **The team has just had a leadership change.** Add a fifth category: "Topics that may now be discussable for the first time." This is sometimes the largest category in the session.
- **The team has run this format before.** The facilitator brings the previous session's parked items and asks which, if any, the team is now ready to engage with. Most are not. One usually is.
