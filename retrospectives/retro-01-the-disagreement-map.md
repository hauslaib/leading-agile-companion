# Retrospective format: The disagreement map

## When to use this

Use this when the team's retrospectives have stopped producing surprises. Discussions are polite, action items are reasonable, and nothing changes between PIs. The signal is not anger or frustration; it is the absence of disagreement in a setting where some disagreement should be present.

This format is poorly suited to teams in active conflict. Teams that are already openly fighting need a different intervention. This is for teams that have settled into agreement that is not real.

Allow 90 minutes. Smaller is fine; shorter is not.

## What this is for

The format externalises disagreements that team members hold privately but have stopped raising. The goal is not to resolve the disagreements in the session. The goal is to make them visible and to identify which ones are worth working on next.

## What you need

- A whiteboard or wall, divided into four columns labelled **Agreement**, **Mild disagreement**, **Strong disagreement**, and **I don't know what others think**.
- Sticky notes, one colour per participant. The colour assignment matters; it is part of the format.
- A facilitator who is not the line manager of anyone in the room.

## Format

### Opening, 5 minutes

Read out the framing without softening it: *"The premise of this session is that we have stopped surfacing disagreement. That is not a personal failing. It is a thing teams settle into. We are going to externalise it."*

Do not invite responses to the framing. Move directly into the first activity.

### Round one: silent generation, 15 minutes

Each participant writes, on their own sticky notes, statements they believe are *true about how the team works* but suspect others may not agree with. The statements should be specific. *"We don't really do code review"* is useful; *"our process is broken"* is not.

Aim for between five and ten statements per participant. Time-box hard. People who finish early do not move on; they sit with the silence.

### Round two: placement, 15 minutes

One participant at a time, in any order, places their notes on the wall under the column they think *most other people in the room* would place them in. Not where they themselves stand, but where they predict the room is.

No discussion during placement. The facilitator's only job is to keep the room from talking.

### Round three: the surprise, 20 minutes

Now the discussion opens, but only on a specific question: *"Which placements surprised you, and why?"*

The interesting data is the gap between where one person predicted the room would land and where the room actually landed. That gap reveals what each person believes is undiscussable. Surface the gaps. Do not try to resolve the underlying disagreements yet.

The facilitator's job here is to prevent the conversation from collapsing into either of two failure modes: a debate about who is right, or a group hug about how it is fine that we disagree. Neither is the point.

### Round four: selection, 20 minutes

From the items now on the wall in the **Strong disagreement** and **I don't know what others think** columns, the team selects no more than three to engage with in the next two iterations. The selection criteria are:

1. The disagreement is about something the team can actually act on. Disagreements about company strategy do not qualify.
2. The cost of the disagreement remaining hidden is concrete. The team can name what is going wrong because of it.
3. At least two people in the room are willing to be in the working conversation about it.

Items not selected are photographed, captured in the team's retro log, and revisited. They are not discarded.

### Closing, 15 minutes

For each selected item, name the next conversation that needs to happen, who is in it, and when. The next conversation is not a meeting to "discuss" the item. It is a working session to take a position on it.

The session ends without action items in the traditional sense. The action is that three previously unspoken disagreements are now spoken, scheduled, and assigned.

## Facilitation notes

The first time a team runs this, the **Agreement** column will be the largest. That is not because there is widespread agreement. It is because participants have not yet built the trust to put items elsewhere. Run the format twice, six weeks apart. The second run usually surfaces what the first one did not.

A common failure mode is the facilitator trying to "make the session safe" with extensive ground rules at the start. Don't. Long preambles signal that something dangerous is about to happen, which is the opposite of what the format is trying to achieve. Open briefly. Move into the work.

Another failure mode: the facilitator validates each surfaced disagreement with affirming language ("that's a really important point"). Stop. Validation in this context functions as a closing move; it tells the room the topic has been received and processed. The format depends on disagreements being received without being neutralised.

If the most senior person in the room places nearly all their items in **Agreement**, that is data. It usually means the rest of the room is calibrating to them, and the format will not produce its full value until the senior person is given a way to be wrong without losing standing. That is not solvable in this session. Note it and address it elsewhere.

## What this format does not do

- It does not produce action items in the conventional sense. If a stakeholder asks for the action items list, share what was scheduled rather than what was decided.
- It does not improve team morale in the short term. Teams typically leave the first run of this format somewhat unsettled. That is expected. Morale recovers once the surfaced disagreements are seen to be addressed seriously.
- It does not work without a facilitator with standing. A peer facilitator can run it. A line manager facilitating their own team usually cannot.
