# Stakeholder tension grid: example

A worked example of the stakeholder tension grid (`worksheets/stakeholder-tension-grid.md`), filled in against Scenario 02: *The skeptical senior engineer*. The example is one possible filled version, completed by the RTE in the third month of the role. It is not the model answer; it is one practitioner's reading.

---

| Label | Role | Seniority |
|---|---|---|
| RTE | The author of the worksheet, two months in role |  |
| M. | Senior engineer, twelve years tenured, technically respected | Peer in role; senior in standing |
| LM | M.'s line manager, the engineering manager for M.'s team | Peer |
| AR | The ART architect | Peer |
| J1 | Junior engineer on M.'s team, mirroring her detachment | Junior |
| J2 | Junior engineer on M.'s team, also mirroring | Junior |

---

## 3. Asymmetric perceptions

| Pair | Asymmetry |
|---|---|
| RTE ↔ M. | The RTE believes a working relationship is in early formation. M. likely treats the RTE as the latest in a sequence of process roles she has watched come and go. |
| M. ↔ LM | LM treats M. as a high-performing senior engineer she manages. M. likely treats LM as a colleague she protects from administrative overhead, not as a manager whose authority she is engaged with. |
| AR ↔ M. | AR treats M. as a peer technical voice. M. respects AR but has not engaged with him on the architectural drift the RTE has noticed. |
| J1, J2 ↔ M. | The juniors look up to M. and read her posture as informed cynicism. M. is not actively mentoring them in this; her posture is being copied without intent. |

The first asymmetry is the most consequential. It explains why the 1:1s have been content-free: I think we are starting; she thinks we are passing through.

---

## 4. Coalitions and triangles

**Coalitions**

| Coalition members | What they tend to align on |
|---|---|
| LM, AR, PO | Operational decisions about the team; broadly trust each other; tend to converge in tri-laterals |
| M., J1, J2 | Posture in ceremonies; technical decisions inside the team |

**Triangles**

| The two parties | The third party they route through | What they are avoiding by not communicating directly |
|---|---|---|
| AR ↔ M. | LM | A direct conversation about the architectural drift would be uncomfortable. LM softens the topic when relaying it. |
| RTE ↔ M. | LM | The RTE has more substantive conversations *about* M. with LM than *with* M. The triangle is forming and should not be allowed to set. |

The second triangle is the one to disrupt. If it persists past PI 4, the RTE-to-M. relationship will have been mediated by LM for so long that direct engagement becomes harder, not easier.

---

## 5. The standing map

| Stakeholder | Authority | Relationship | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| RTE | Moderate (process) | Weak with M., moderate elsewhere | Cannot lead an intervention with M. directly yet |
| LM | Strong (line) | Strong with M. | Is the right person to surface concerns about M.'s position to M., but only if she chooses to, and only on the right footing |
| AR | None over M.'s engagement | Moderate | Could open a substantive technical conversation with M. that incidentally surfaces engagement |
| PO | None over engagement | Moderate | Not a fit for this intervention |

Reading: the right next step is not an RTE intervention with M. It is a conversation between the RTE and LM about whether LM is willing to open a particular kind of conversation with M., and on what terms. If LM is not willing or not the right person, the second-best path is through AR via a technical conversation.

---

## 6. What this grid shows you

The most useful thing the grid surfaces is that the RTE has been attempting to build a relationship with M. as if it were a peer-to-peer process, when M.'s seniority of standing makes it asymmetric in a way the RTE has not been treating seriously. The triangle through LM is hardening. The juniors copying M.'s posture are a symptom worth watching but not the place to intervene; addressing them around M. would be visible to the room and read as undercutting. The architect is the most underused asset in the situation and has not been considered as a partner in the question.

---

## 7. What the grid is missing

The grid does not capture M.'s history with the previous three transformations, which is almost certainly the dominant variable in her current posture. It does not capture whether LM has previously tried to address M.'s disengagement and given up. It does not capture the broader engineering culture, which may be reinforcing M.'s position in ways no individual intervention can change. The off-grid influence I am most aware of is the head of engineering, who hired M. twelve years ago and whose informal endorsement carries more weight in M.'s calibration than anyone currently on the grid.

---

*This example is illustrative. The same scenario, filled in by a different RTE, would produce a recognisably different grid. That is the format working as intended.*
