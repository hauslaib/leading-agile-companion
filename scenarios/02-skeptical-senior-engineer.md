# Scenario 02 — The skeptical senior engineer

## Setup

A new RTE has been in role for two months on an ART of seven teams. One of the teams contains a senior engineer — call her M. — with twelve years at the company. M. is technically respected across the ART, mentors several junior engineers, and has seen three previous "agile transformations" come and go. She is polite in ceremonies, attends what is required, and contributes nothing beyond what is asked.

In retrospectives, when prompted, M. offers measured observations like "the process is fine" or "we'll see how it goes". In PI planning, she does her team's work competently and goes home on time. She has not openly resisted anything. She has also not endorsed anything.

Her team's velocity is stable. Their PI objectives are met. By every dashboard metric, the team is healthy.

The RTE notices three things over the second month:

1. Two junior engineers on M.'s team have started mirroring her detachment in ceremonies, going through the motions without engaging.
2. M. is the team's de facto technical decision-maker, and her decisions occasionally cut against the architectural direction set by the ART without anyone, including the architect, raising it.
3. When the RTE has tried short one-on-one conversations with M., the responses have been cordial, brief, and content-free.

The team is not failing, but something is nonetheless wrong.

## The tensions in play

- **Compliance without commitment.** M. is not violating any expectation. The dysfunction is in what is absent, not what is present, which makes it hard to name.
- **Earned skepticism.** M. has watched three transformations fail. Her detachment may be the rational response of someone protecting her energy. Treating it as a problem to fix can be condescending.
- **Influence without authority.** M.'s technical credibility gives her organisational weight that her job title does not capture. Her disengagement spreads; her engagement would too.
- **The RTE's standing.** Two months in, the RTE has not yet earned the right to ask M. to invest more. Pushing too early entrenches the dynamic.
- **Metrics that don't see this.** Every quantitative signal says the team is fine. The problem is visible only to someone watching the room, not the dashboard.

## What is *not* the problem

- M.'s performance. She is doing her job to the standard expected.
- The team's delivery. They are meeting commitments.
- Any single ceremony. Improving the format of retrospectives will not change M.'s posture.
- Authority. The RTE could escalate to M.'s line manager, but that almost certainly makes things worse.

## Discussion prompts

1. What is the RTE actually trying to achieve here? Articulate the desired end state in one sentence without using the word "engagement".
2. M.'s detachment may be a reasonable response to her history. Under what conditions is it the RTE's place to try to change it, and under what conditions is it not?
3. The two junior engineers are mirroring M. Does the RTE address this through M., around M., or directly with them? What are the trade-offs?
4. What would it take for M. to trust that this transformation is different from the previous three? Is the RTE in a position to provide that, or does it have to come from elsewhere?
5. The RTE's one-on-ones with M. have been content-free. Is that because the RTE is asking the wrong questions, because M. is choosing not to engage, or because there is no relationship yet to carry the conversation? How would you tell which?
6. Imagine the RTE does nothing for the next PI. What changes, what stays the same, and is "nothing" actually a defensible choice?

## Facilitator notes

This scenario reliably exposes participants' default theory of why people disengage. Some will assume M. is burned out and needs care. Others will assume she is cynical and needs to be challenged. Others will assume she is fine and the RTE is over-reading the room. All three readings are plausible from the setup, and the scenario does not resolve which is correct -- deliberately.

The richest discussions come from asking participants what evidence they would need to gather *before* choosing an intervention. Many will skip straight to "I would have a deeper one-on-one with her", which is a tactic in search of a diagnosis.

A trap to watch for: framing M. as a problem the RTE must solve. M. is a person, not a process defect. Participants who frame her in instrumental terms ("how do I get her to…") tend to propose interventions that M. would correctly perceive as manipulation. Push the framing back toward what the RTE actually owes M., versus what M. owes the ART.

Pair with the stakeholder-tension grid (`worksheets/stakeholder-tension-grid.md`) for participants to map M.'s position relative to the RTE, the team, and the broader ART.

## Variations

- **M. is leaving in three months.** Does the calculation change?
- **M. has just been passed over for a promotion.** Does that information belong in the RTE's thinking, or is it private to M.?
- **M. is the only senior engineer on a team of juniors.** Her disengagement now has a development cost the team cannot absorb.

Each variation tightens the time pressure or raises the stakes, and tends to produce different proposed interventions from the same participants, which is itself worth surfacing in the debrief.
