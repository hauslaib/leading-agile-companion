# Scenario 03 — The executive promise

## Setup

At a customer advisory board meeting, a senior executive — call him R. — committed publicly to delivering a major capability "by the end of Q3". The capability sits across two ARTs in the solution train. R. did not consult either RTE before making the commitment. He did not consult the system architect. He did consult the head of product management, who nodded but later said she had been answering a different question.

The commitment is now in the customer's quarterly roadmap deck. R. has mentioned it twice in all-hands. Marketing has begun drafting launch material.

The two RTEs, working with their architects, estimate the capability at roughly twice the available capacity for the period, assuming nothing else slips. To hit Q3 they would have to descope two PI objectives that another executive — call her S. — has already committed to a different customer.

R. believes the train is protecting capacity. The RTEs believe R. has overcommitted. The head of product management believes she is being made the scapegoat for a conversation she did not actually have. S. has not yet been told that her commitments are at risk.

The STE finds out about the commitment from a customer-facing slide deck forwarded by an account manager.

## The tensions in play

- **Public commitment vs. private capacity.** Once a commitment is in a customer deck, the cost of retracting it is paid in trust, not just in roadmap mechanics. That cost is real, even when the commitment was made carelessly.
- **Two executives, one capacity pool.** R. and S. have made promises that cannot both be kept. Neither knows yet that they are competing.
- **Information asymmetry as leverage.** The STE currently knows more than any individual executive. Holding that information is a temporary form of authority that decays the moment it is shared.
- **The head of product management's position.** She is being implicitly cited as the source of the commitment. Correcting that publicly damages R.; staying silent damages her.
- **Truthful framing without naming a culprit.** Any conversation that resolves this requires saying, in some form, "the commitment was made without the data". Saying that without making R. defensive is the actual skill.

## What is *not* the problem

- The estimate. Both ARTs and the architect agree on the numbers. Re-estimating will not produce a different answer.
- The capability itself. It is a reasonable thing to build, eventually.
- R.'s authority to make commitments. He has it. The question is what to do now that one has been made.
- The teams. They have not yet been pulled into this and should not be until the framing is decided.

## Discussion prompts

1. The STE has the most complete picture of the conflict and the least formal authority to resolve it. Is that a problem to escape or a position to use? What does using it well look like?
2. Three executives need to be in the same conversation: R., S., and the head of product management. Who calls that meeting, and what is the agenda framing that lets all three save face?
3. The customer has been told Q3. There are roughly four ways to handle that: deliver, descope and re-promise, slip honestly, or split the capability into a Q3-shippable subset and a later remainder. Walk through what each costs and who bears the cost.
4. The head of product management has been quietly mis-cited. Does the STE address this directly with her, raise it with R., or leave it for her to handle? What is the STE's standing to intervene in something that is technically not their problem?
5. If the STE does nothing for two weeks, the situation gets worse in specific ways. Name them. Then name what gets worse if the STE moves too quickly.
6. After this is resolved, what mechanism would have to exist for an executive commitment of this scale to surface to the train *before* it reaches a customer deck? Who owns that mechanism?

## Facilitator notes

This scenario tends to produce two opposing first instincts. The first is to "go fix it" — convene the executives, run the numbers, force a decision. The second is to "stay in lane" — surface the conflict to the relevant accountable parties and let them resolve it. Neither is wrong; both have failure modes worth surfacing.

The "go fix it" instinct often underestimates how much of the problem is political rather than technical. The capacity arithmetic is the easy part. The harder part is letting R. retract the commitment without losing face, and that is not the STE's job to engineer.

The "stay in lane" instinct often overestimates how cleanly the accountable parties will pick the problem up if it is dropped on them in isolation. Without the STE assembling the picture, R. and S. may never realise they are competing for the same capacity until two customers are unhappy.

A common trap: participants propose escalating to the executive above R. and S. as the cleanest path. Push back. That move solves the problem at the cost of teaching R. that the train escalates rather than partners. The trade-off may still be worth it, but it should be made deliberately, not by default.

Pair with the decision-log worksheet (`worksheets/decision-log.md`). The decision being made here will need to be documented carefully, including what was descoped and why, because it will be referenced in future planning conversations whether or not the STE wants it to be.

## Variations

- **The customer is the company's largest account.** The cost of retracting the commitment is meaningfully higher and may not be recoverable.
- **R. is new in role.** The commitment was a rookie error rather than a pattern. The intervention shifts toward coaching rather than containment.
- **The capability is partially built.** A previous PI included foundational work, and roughly 30% of the capability is already in production. The conversation about descoping changes shape entirely.
