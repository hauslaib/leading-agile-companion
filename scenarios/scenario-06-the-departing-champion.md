# Scenario 06 — The departing champion

## Setup

A product manager — call her L. — has resigned. She has eight weeks of notice. Over four years she has become the connective tissue between the business side and the engineering organisation: she runs the quarterly stakeholder review that every executive attends; she has the relationships with the three external customers whose feedback shapes roadmap decisions; she is the only person who knows why the architectural decision in the third PI was made the way it was, and that decision is now being revisited.

Her replacement has not been named. Hiring is open externally. HR estimates a realistic timeline of three to four months from open req to onboarded.

The RTE is one of three people L. has briefed in detail about her current commitments. The architect and L.'s direct manager are the others. Each of them holds a different slice of what L. carries. None of them holds it all.

In the first week after the announcement:

- Two stakeholders have asked the RTE who their new point of contact is. The RTE does not have an answer.
- A roadmap commitment that L. negotiated with the largest customer is due for a quarterly review, and L. has indicated she will not be attending the review held two weeks after her last day.
- L.'s manager has scheduled a knowledge-transfer session and has set aside two hours.
- Two engineers have, separately, asked the RTE what this means for the platform initiative L. championed.

The team is professional. There has been no drama. Things are also, visibly, beginning to come unstuck in ways that are not yet legible to anyone above L.'s manager.

## The tensions in play

- **Knowledge that lives in relationships, not documents.** The most valuable things L. holds are not transferable in a two-hour session because they are not knowledge in that sense. They are standing, trust, and history with specific people.
- **Two-hour transfer vs. four-month gap.** Even an excellent transfer cannot bridge the staffing gap. Treating the transfer as the solution underestimates the gap; treating the gap as unsolvable abandons the transfer.
- **The interim distribution problem.** L.'s responsibilities will need to be carried by a combination of the architect, the manager, and the RTE for at least three months. None of them has capacity. None of them can refuse.
- **Continuity of commitments.** The roadmap commitments L. made were partly underwritten by her personal credibility with stakeholders. That credibility does not transfer with the role.
- **The risk of pretending nothing has changed.** The temptation to project stability ("we have a plan, we are covered") to stakeholders is high and almost always counterproductive. Stakeholders can usually tell.

## What is *not* the problem

- L.'s decision. She is leaving for legitimate reasons. The conversation about retention should not be reopened.
- The hiring timeline. Three to four months is what it is. Compressing it produces a worse hire.
- The team's morale. They are stable. Stability now is not a guarantee of stability in two months, but it is also not the thing to fix this week.
- L.'s manager. She is doing the right things in the right order.

## Discussion prompts

1. The two-hour knowledge transfer is real but insufficient. List the three categories of things L. carries that *cannot* be transferred in such a session. Then propose a distinct mechanism for each.
2. The RTE has been asked by two stakeholders who their new contact is. What is the right answer this week? What is the right answer in six weeks? What changes between those two answers?
3. The roadmap commitment L. negotiated will be reviewed without her. Should the RTE be the one in the room, or should L.'s manager, or both? What does each option signal to the customer?
4. L. championed the platform initiative. Without her, the initiative may lose stakeholder support. Is that a problem to fight, or a signal that the initiative was always more fragile than the team realised?
5. The architect, the manager, and the RTE each hold a slice of L.'s context. They have not yet sat down together to assemble the full picture. Is that meeting the RTE's to call? What is the agenda?
6. Imagine L. is replaced in four months by someone capable but new to the company and the relationships. What can be put in place now so the new person inherits a function rather than a wreckage?

## Facilitator notes

This scenario tends to expose participants' relationship to organisational fragility. Some will frame departures like this as an inevitable feature of organisational life that mature programmes plan for. Others will frame it as a failure of succession planning that should not have been allowed to happen. Both framings are partially right, and the productive question is what either framing implies for what to do *now*, not what should have been done eighteen months ago.

A trap to watch for: the proposed solution focuses entirely on documentation. "L. should write everything down" is reasonable but vastly overestimates how much of her value is documentable. Push participants to articulate what specifically would be in the document and what would not, and watch the second list grow longer than the first.

Another trap: assuming the right answer is to compress hiring. Participants will sometimes propose internal promotions, contractor cover, or accelerated external hires. Each has genuine costs that are easy to dismiss in the moment. Press on what each costs at the twelve-month horizon, not the three-month one.

A useful late-discussion prompt: ask whether the RTE's primary obligation in this scenario is to L., to the team, to the stakeholders, or to the next product manager. The answer is usually all four, but in different proportions, and articulating the proportions clarifies the next move.

Pair with the decision-log worksheet (`worksheets/decision-log.md`) for the decisions about interim coverage and roadmap commitments that will need to be made and revisited as the situation develops.

## Variations

- **L. is willing to remain available informally for three months after she leaves.** This is generous and creates its own problem: the interim arrangement becomes load-bearing in a way that cannot be relied on.
- **L.'s replacement has been named internally and is starting next week.** The transfer problem is real but bounded. The variation surfaces what the RTE owes the new product manager that L. could not provide.
- **L. is leaving because she lost a conflict over the platform initiative she championed.** Her departure is not separable from the work, and the team will read it that way whether or not anyone says so.
